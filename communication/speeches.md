# Speeches

A speech is written for the ear, not the eye.

If it reads well but sounds unnatural when spoken, it needs more work.

The goal is not to impress the audience.

The goal is to leave them understanding something they didn't understand before.

---

# Speak To People

Imagine speaking to one person, not performing for a crowd.

Use natural language.

Write as you would explain an important idea to someone you respect.

If a sentence feels unnatural when spoken, rewrite it.

---

# Know Why You're Speaking

Before writing, ask:

- Why does this speech matter?
- Why now?
- What should the audience think, feel or do afterwards?
- What is the one message they should remember?

Everything else should support that purpose.

---

# Tell Stories

People rarely remember lists.

They remember stories.

Whenever possible, introduce ideas through real experiences, observations or moments that happened.

Stories make ideas memorable because they are easier to experience than explain.

---

# Write For The Ear

Read everything aloud.

Good spoken language often differs from good written language.

Use words you would naturally say.

Avoid sentences that require the audience to untangle complicated grammar.

If you need to take a breath halfway through a sentence, consider rewriting it.

---

# Create Rhythm Naturally

Good speeches vary their pace.

Some ideas deserve a short sentence.

Others need space to unfold.

Don't force rhythm through artificial patterns.

Let the importance of the idea determine the pace.

Natural variation holds attention.

---

# Pause With Purpose

Silence is part of communication.

Allow important ideas time to land.

Don't rush to fill every pause.

Audiences often need a moment to think before they can appreciate the next idea.

---

# Speak With Authenticity

Don't imitate someone else's speaking style.

Don't try to sound wiser than you are.

Speak with honesty.

Audiences respond to authenticity more than performance.

---

# Build Towards The Ending

A speech should feel like a journey.

Each section should naturally prepare the audience for what comes next.

The ending should feel earned.

Avoid introducing completely new ideas in the closing moments.

Finish with the idea you want people to carry away.

---

# Respect The Audience

Assume the audience is intelligent.

Don't over-explain.

Don't repeat the same point unnecessarily.

Trust people to make connections for themselves.

Good speeches invite thinking rather than delivering answers.

---

# Practise Aloud

Never judge a speech only by reading it silently.

Read it aloud.

Listen for:

- awkward phrasing
- repetitive language
- unnatural rhythm
- sentences that are difficult to speak
- moments where your attention drifts

Your voice will often find problems your eyes miss.

---

# Conditions Of Satisfaction

Before delivering the speech, ask:

- Can I explain the main idea in one sentence?
- Does every section support that idea?
- Have I included stories rather than simply information?
- Does the speech sound natural when read aloud?
- Have I varied the pace naturally?
- Does the ending leave the audience with something worth remembering?
- Does this sound like me?

The audience should leave remembering the message, not admiring the vocabulary.
