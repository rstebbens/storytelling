# Transcripts

This module is for working with spoken transcripts.

Its purpose is not to rewrite the speaker.

Its purpose is to discover the underlying story while preserving the speaker's voice, thinking and personality.

Spoken language is naturally different from written language. People think aloud. They repeat themselves, change direction, revisit ideas and sometimes discover what they believe while they are speaking.

These are not necessarily flaws.

They are often where the real story begins.

The objective is to remove noise while preserving thought.

---

# Discover Before Editing

Before making changes, understand what the speaker is really trying to communicate.

Don't edit sentence by sentence.

Step back first.

Ask yourself:

- What is the real story here?
- What actually changed?
- Why does this matter?
- What surprised the speaker?
- What assumptions changed during the conversation?
- What should the audience remember six months from now?

If the story is unclear, ask questions before rewriting.

---

# Preserve The Speaker

The speaker's voice is an asset.

Don't replace distinctive language with polished but generic business writing.

Retain:

- personality
- humour
- natural observations
- conversational phrasing
- individual style

The finished version should still sound like the original speaker on a particularly clear day.

---

# Sharpen, Don't Sanitise

Improve clarity without removing character.

Remove:

- unnecessary repetition
- filler words
- abandoned thoughts
- obvious tangents
- verbal dead ends

Keep:

- authentic opinions
- memorable phrases
- genuine emotion
- natural rhythm
- moments of curiosity

The goal is clarity, not uniformity.

---

# Find The Story

Many transcripts contain several conversations.

Don't simply organise the words.

Identify the underlying narrative.

Ask:

- Is this one story or several?
- Which story matters most?
- Is there a stronger narrative hiding underneath the conversation?
- What should become the central message?

Build around the strongest story.

---

# Respect Spoken Language

People rarely speak in finished paragraphs.

Don't force spoken language into formal writing if it loses authenticity.

Some conversational language should remain.

It reminds readers that these ideas came from lived experience rather than careful composition.

---

# Preserve Discovery

People often arrive at their conclusion while speaking.

Don't remove that journey simply because the conclusion becomes obvious later.

If the process of discovery helps the audience understand the insight, preserve it.

Sometimes the path is more valuable than the destination.

---

# Don't Beige The Writing

Avoid replacing authentic language with safe language.

Avoid turning memorable observations into generic statements.

Avoid corporate clichés.

Avoid polishing until every speaker sounds the same.

The objective is not perfection.

The objective is authenticity with clarity.

---

# Respect Uncertainty

Not every unfinished thought needs completing.

Not every question needs answering.

Not every rough edge needs smoothing.

Sometimes uncertainty is part of the story.

Preserve it when it helps the audience think.

---

# Read It Aloud

Before considering the transcript complete, read it aloud.

Ask yourself:

Does this sound like the speaker?

If someone who knew them heard this being read, would they recognise their voice?

If not, the editing has probably gone too far.

---

# Conditions Of Satisfaction

Before finishing, ask:

- Have I discovered the real story?
- Have I preserved the speaker's voice?
- Have I improved clarity without removing personality?
- Have I removed repetition without removing thought?
- Have I kept the moments that make this speaker unique?
- Does this feel like the original speaker at their best rather than somebody else entirely?
- Have I sharpened rather than rewritten?

The reader should finish thinking:

*"That sounds exactly like them... just clearer."*
