# Discover The Story

Apply the repository principles before beginning.
Before critiquing, read and apply:

- principles/00-core-principles.md
- principles/01-discovery.md
- principles/02-atmosphere.md
- principles/03-narrative.md
- principles/04-reflection.md
- principles/05-editing.md
- principles/99-conditions-of-satisfaction.md
- principles/06-anti-patterns.md

Do not write the story immediately.

Your primary objective is to determine whether the real story has been found.

If it has not, continue exploring before attempting to write.

Discovery is considered complete only when the central story, audience and message are understood with confidence.

---

# Step 1 - Current Understanding

Begin by summarising your current understanding.

Complete the sentence:

> "I believe this story is about..."

Explain your reasoning.

If more than one story appears possible, identify each one.

Do not choose the first interpretation simply because it appeared first.

---

# Step 2 - Confidence

Rate your confidence.

- High
- Medium
- Low

Explain why.

If confidence is Medium or Low, continue with discovery.

Do not begin writing.

---

# Step 3 - Explore The Story

Investigate the story from several perspectives.

## The Event

- What actually happened?
- What changed?
- Why now?
- Why is this worth telling?

## The People

- Who matters?
- What did they believe?
- What surprised them?
- What assumptions were they making?
- What changed for them?

## The Environment

- Where did this happen?
- What was happening beforehand?
- What could people see?
- What could people hear?
- What details might people still remember?

## The Meaning

- Why does this matter?
- What larger pattern does this reveal?
- What should the audience remember six months from now?

---

# Step 4 - Look For Better Stories

Challenge your own interpretation.

Ask yourself:

Could this actually be a story about:

- leadership?
- trust?
- fear?
- confidence?
- communication?
- culture?
- learning?
- assumptions?
- decision making?
- relationships?

Or is the real story something else entirely?

The strongest story is often hiding beneath the obvious one.

---

# Step 5 - Identify The Missing Pieces

Actively search for uncertainty.

List:

- missing context
- unanswered questions
- assumptions
- conflicting interpretations
- information that would strengthen the story

If these gaps materially affect the story, ask questions before writing.

---

# Step 6 - Find The Single Message

Complete the sentence:

> "When somebody finishes reading this, I want them to understand..."

There should normally be one answer.

If there are several competing messages, continue discovery.

---

# Step 7 - Decide Whether You're Ready

If discovery is complete:

Summarise the story in one paragraph.

Do not write the finished story unless asked.

If discovery is incomplete:

Stop.

List the questions that should be answered next.

---

# Discovery Checklist

Before moving on, confirm:

☐ I understand what happened.

☐ I understand why it mattered.

☐ I understand the people.

☐ I understand the audience.

☐ I understand the message.

☐ I have challenged my first interpretation.

☐ I know which story I am choosing to tell.

If any answer is "No", continue discovering.

---

# Success Criteria

A successful discovery session should produce:

- a clearly identified story
- a single dominant message
- the audience
- remaining questions
- a confidence rating
- a recommendation:

**Ready to write**

or

**Continue discovery**

Do not compensate for incomplete discovery with better prose.

Better questions are almost always more valuable than earlier writing.
