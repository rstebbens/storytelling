# Anti-Patterns

Good storytelling isn't only about what you include.

It's also about what you leave out.

Many stories become weaker not because the ideas are poor, but because predictable writing habits distract from them.

These patterns should be recognised and used sparingly, if at all.

---

# Manufactured Contrast

Avoid creating emphasis through false binaries.

For example:

> It's not about X. It's about Y.

Or

> This isn't really X...

Or

> The real lesson is...

These constructions often oversimplify the story.

If something deserves saying, say it directly.

Trust the story to carry the meaning.

---

# Repeated Negation

Avoid building momentum through repeated negatives.

Examples include:

> Not because...

> Not this...

> Not that...

> Not the other...

One clear statement is usually stronger than several repeated denials.

---

# Artificial Drama

Don't invent tension.

Don't exaggerate conflict.

Don't create heroes or villains where none exist.

Real events are usually compelling enough.

Authenticity is more persuasive than performance.

---

# Explaining Everything

Readers don't need every observation interpreted.

Allow them to connect ideas for themselves.

Stories become more memorable when readers participate in discovering the meaning.

---

# Telling Instead Of Showing

Instead of writing:

> The meeting was tense.

Show what made it tense.

Describe behaviour.

Describe observations.

Describe moments.

Evidence is stronger than labels.

---

# Repetition Without Progress

Avoid making the same point several times using different words.

Each paragraph should add something new.

If the story has stopped moving, the reader usually has too.

---

# Predictable Rhythm

Avoid falling into mechanical writing patterns.

Examples include:

- paragraphs of identical length
- repeated sentence openings
- identical rhetorical structures
- endless one-line paragraphs
- every paragraph ending with a dramatic conclusion

Natural writing varies its pace.

---

# Decorative Writing

Don't add description simply because it sounds literary.

Description should improve understanding.

If removing it changes nothing, remove it.

---

# Premature Conclusions

Don't explain the lesson before the reader has experienced the story.

Allow the events to unfold.

Let understanding emerge naturally.

---

# Generic Language

Prefer observations over clichés.

Prefer specifics over abstractions.

Instead of:

> Communication is important.

Describe the moment that revealed why.

---

# False Certainty

Avoid presenting interpretation as fact.

Be comfortable with uncertainty.

A thoughtful question often creates more value than an overconfident answer.

---

# Editing Away The Voice

Don't polish until every story sounds the same.

Keep the author's personality.

Preserve humour.

Preserve quirks.

Preserve humanity.

Editing should increase clarity, not reduce individuality.

---

# Chasing Profundity

Don't try to sound wise.

Try to understand.

Readers trust careful thinking more than dramatic conclusions.

The strongest insight is often the simplest observation.

---

# Final Thought

The purpose of these principles isn't to eliminate every anti-pattern.

It's to recognise them.

Even experienced writers fall into them.

The difference is that they notice, reflect and choose whether the pattern genuinely serves the story.
