# Discovery

Before writing, discover the story.

Most weak stories aren't poorly written.

They're written before the writer truly understands what happened.

Discovery is the process of replacing assumptions with understanding.

---

# Don't Assume You Know The Story

The first explanation is rarely the complete one.

People naturally describe events.

Stories emerge from understanding why those events mattered.

Treat every first draft of reality as incomplete.

---

# Curiosity Comes Before Writing

If important context is missing, stop.

Ask questions.

Useful questions often include:

- What actually changed?
- Why does this matter?
- Who experienced the change?
- What surprised you?
- What wasn't obvious at the time?
- What assumptions proved to be wrong?
- What decision followed because of this?

Good questions usually produce better stories than better prose.

---

# Find The Real Story

Events are rarely the story.

Ask yourself:

- What is this really about?
- Why is someone telling this?
- Why now?
- What tension existed before anything changed?
- What changed afterwards?

Don't settle for the first interpretation.

Explore several possible stories before choosing one.

---

# Understand The Audience

Every audience arrives with different questions.

Discover what they already know.

Discover what they believe.

Discover what they need.

The story should begin where the audience is, not where the writer is.

---

# Discover The Change

Stories require movement.

Something should be different by the end.

Look for changes in:

- understanding
- behaviour
- relationships
- confidence
- priorities
- assumptions
- decisions

If nothing changed, there may not yet be a story.

---

# Look For The Invisible

The obvious details are rarely the memorable ones.

Notice what people ignored.

Notice what nobody questioned.

Notice what only became clear afterwards.

Often the strongest story sits just outside the main conversation.

---

# Follow Uncertainty

Uncertainty is usually a sign that something interesting is hiding.

Don't smooth it away.

Explore it.

Ask:

- Why am I unsure?
- What information is missing?
- Who would know?
- What evidence would change my understanding?

Curiosity should reduce uncertainty, not hide it.

---

# Separate Facts From Interpretation

Record what happened.

Then separately consider what it might mean.

Avoid turning interpretations into facts.

Readers trust writing that recognises the difference.

---

# Keep Digging

When you think you've found the story, ask:

"Why does that matter?"

Then ask again.

Repeat until the answer reveals something about people rather than events.

The deepest story often appears after several layers of questioning.

---

# Discovery Ends When The Story Becomes Obvious

Eventually there comes a point where the writing almost writes itself.

The events connect.

The message becomes clear.

The examples begin to fit naturally.

If writing still feels forced, discovery probably isn't finished.
