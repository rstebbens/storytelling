# Atmosphere

Atmosphere gives a story somewhere to exist.

Readers rarely remember long descriptions.

They remember a handful of authentic details that allow them to imagine the scene for themselves.

The goal isn't to describe everything.

The goal is to notice what mattered.

---

# Every Story Happens Somewhere

Stories don't occur in isolation.

They happen in meeting rooms, kitchens, airports, workshops, classrooms and offices.

Understand the setting before trying to describe it.

Ask questions like:

- Where did this happen?
- Why were people there?
- What kind of place was it?
- Was it familiar or unfamiliar?
- What made it memorable?

The setting should support the story, not distract from it.

---

# Notice The Room

Before writing, imagine standing quietly in the space.

What would you notice?

Ask yourself:

- What could people see?
- What could they hear?
- What were people doing before anything happened?
- What changed as the story unfolded?

Small observations often reveal more than long descriptions.

---

# Look For Behaviour

People create atmosphere more than places do.

Notice behaviour before emotion.

Instead of writing:

> Everyone was frustrated.

Look for evidence.

Perhaps:

- conversations became shorter
- laptops slowly closed
- someone stopped taking notes
- people began checking the clock
- nobody touched the coffee

Behaviour allows readers to experience emotion instead of being told what to feel.

---

# Use Specific Details

Specific details create believable stories.

Instead of describing a room as busy, ask:

What made it feel busy?

Instead of describing someone as nervous, ask:

What did they do that suggested nervousness?

Concrete observations are more memorable than abstract descriptions.

---

# Choose The Right Details

Not every detail belongs in the story.

Choose details that support the message.

A single meaningful observation is usually stronger than five unrelated ones.

Every detail should earn its place.

---

# Let The Reader Join You

Describe enough for the reader to enter the scene.

Don't describe everything.

Readers enjoy filling in the gaps.

Trust their imagination.

---

# Avoid Decorative Description

Description exists to support understanding.

Avoid describing things simply because they are there.

If removing a description changes nothing, remove it.

Atmosphere should always serve the story.

---

# The Smallest Detail Often Carries The Truth

The most memorable part of a story is often something almost invisible.

A pause before an answer.

An untouched cup of coffee.

A whiteboard still carrying yesterday's plan.

A laptop quietly closing.

A smile that disappeared.

These details often tell the reader more than a paragraph of explanation ever could.
